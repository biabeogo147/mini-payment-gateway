\chapter{Database Design}

\section{Entity Model}

The database uses PostgreSQL with SQLAlchemy models and Alembic migrations. The
schema is designed to support merchant lifecycle management, credentials,
payment attempts, refunds, webhook delivery, provider callback evidence,
reconciliation, audit logging, internal users, and merchant portal users.

\section{Database Invariants}

\begin{longtable}{L{0.33\textwidth}L{0.57\textwidth}}
\caption{Important database invariants}\\
\toprule
\textbf{Invariant} & \textbf{Reason} \\
\midrule
\endfirsthead
\toprule
\textbf{Invariant} & \textbf{Reason} \\
\midrule
\endhead
\texttt{merchants.merchant\_id} is unique. & Public merchant identity must be stable across APIs and dashboards. \\
Only one active credential per merchant. & HMAC verification should have one active secret source at a time. \\
\texttt{merchant\_users} are unique by merchant and email. & A merchant portal email identifies one account within one merchant tenant. \\
One pending payment per merchant and order. & Prevents duplicate active QR payment attempts for the same business order. \\
Refunds are unique by merchant and refund id. & Provides business idempotency for refund creation. \\
At most one refunded row per payment. & Prevents multiple successful full refunds against the same payment. \\
Payment, refund, and webhook rows are indexed by merchant and creation time. & Supports merchant-scoped analytics and explorer queries efficiently. \\
Raw secrets and plaintext passwords are not retrievable through read APIs. & Protects merchant credentials and interactive dashboard accounts. \\
\bottomrule
\end{longtable}

\section{Schema Evolution}

Schema changes are applied through discrete Alembic revisions. The migration
history reflects the system's feature growth: merchant and credential tables,
payments and order references, refunds, webhook persistence, callback and audit
evidence, internal auth and Ops dashboard support, and finally merchant portal
accounts and portal-facing read models.
