\section{Backend Layering}

The backend follows a compact layered architecture. It is intentionally simpler
than full domain-driven design, but it still keeps HTTP handling, schemas,
business rules, persistence, and database concerns separated so that each layer
has one main responsibility.

\section{Business Rules And State Machines}

The domain behavior is centered on explicit state transitions rather than
implicit status changes inside controllers.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Core state models}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{L{0.24\textwidth}L{0.24\textwidth}Y}
\toprule
\textbf{Domain object} & \textbf{Persisted states} & \textbf{Design note} \\
\midrule
Merchant operational status & \texttt{PENDING\_REVIEW}, \texttt{ACTIVE}, \texttt{REJECTED}, \texttt{SUSPENDED}, \texttt{DISABLED} & Operational status remains on \texttt{merchants}, while onboarding approval state is tracked separately on \texttt{merchant\_onboarding\_cases}. \\
Payment transaction & \texttt{PENDING}, \texttt{SUCCESS}, \texttt{FAILED}, \texttt{EXPIRED} & Provider callbacks finalize pending payments, but late or conflicting outcomes create reconciliation evidence instead of reviving invalid state. \\
Refund transaction & \texttt{REFUND\_PENDING}, \texttt{REFUNDED}, \texttt{REFUND\_FAILED} & Only full refunds are implemented; state changes depend on refund rules and provider callback outcomes. \\
Webhook event & \texttt{PENDING}, \texttt{DELIVERED}, \texttt{FAILED} & Delivery success is independent from payment or refund final state. Retry metadata is stored on the event and attempt history is stored separately. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

Two design choices are especially important:

\begin{itemize}
  \item payment expiration does not silently disappear; it creates a durable
  state transition and can emit a webhook event;
  \item ambiguous provider evidence creates a reconciliation record rather than
  forcing a risky automatic correction.
\end{itemize}

\section{Asynchronous Event Lifecycle And Recovery}

The project contains implemented asynchronous service operations even though it
does not yet run a standalone long-running worker process. Expiration and
due-webhook delivery are represented as explicit service operations that can be
invoked by a scheduler or worker trigger.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Merchant payment creation stores a pending payment with expiration
  metadata.
  \item Provider callbacks update payments or refunds, store callback evidence,
  and create durable webhook events when final states are reached.
  \item Expiration service marks overdue pending payments as expired and can
  create \texttt{payment.expired} webhook events.
  \item Webhook delivery service sends signed payloads, stores each HTTP attempt,
  and applies the retry schedule of 1, 5, and 15 minutes before final failure.
  \item Failed or mismatched external evidence produces reconciliation records
  for manual internal review.
  \item Internal users can manually retry eligible failed webhook events without
  mutating the already-final payment or refund state.
\end{enumerate}

This separation makes the design easier to test. Business rules live in service
functions and repositories, while the future scheduler or worker integration can
remain thin and orchestration-focused.
